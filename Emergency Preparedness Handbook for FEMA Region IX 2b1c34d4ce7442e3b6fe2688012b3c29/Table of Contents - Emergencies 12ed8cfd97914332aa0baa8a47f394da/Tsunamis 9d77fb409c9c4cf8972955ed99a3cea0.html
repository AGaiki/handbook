Why are Tsunamis Dangerous?

As mentioned in the explanation, Tsunamis can travel inland at speeds of 20-30 mph. At enormous speeds and sizes, Tsunamis can cause much structural damage to houses and other buildings that may even be crucial in daily life such as hospitals and police stations. More significantly it can take the lives of many individuals who are unfortunate enough to get caught by the waves by either drowning them or causing collisions with larger objects. With the constant withdrawal and resending of waves, many objects can be dragged out with it including larger objects such as cars trees, and electrical wires. This is especially the case because the seismic activity beforehand has to reach 7.5 on the Richter scale, which would, in itself, cause significant damage.

How Can You Prepare for a Tsunami? (In Accordance to Ready.gov)

Something to keep in the back of your mind is that if you either see the water receding out or building up, it is generally too late to outrun it.

Important aspects to consider planning ahead for are:

  • Learning about Tsunami behavior for prior knowledge
    • When visiting an area that is prone to Tsunamis, it is recommended that you talk to the locals in order to acquire information on evacuation plans
      • Come up with your own evacuation plan that may include familial relations, contact out of state, locating a shelter (about 100 feet above sea level;1 mile inland), and areas for regrouping if the event ends up catastrophic
  • Make sure that you are signed up for local emergency alerts and that you keep an ear out for the EAS/NOAA radio broadcast that would indicate any signs of Tsunami activity
  • Consider purchasing an insurance plan that covers damage from floods and earthquakes
  • If there is ever the chance that you may not escape an earthquake locate the tall buildings in your area that you can wait the Tsunami out on

What Can You Do During a Tsunami?

What you can do prior to the initial wave:

  • Tsunamis occur due to large seismic activity, in with that in mind, stay low to the ground during the "earthquake" and protect your head and neck from any falling objects
  • Be cautious of any odd wave behavior and for any "roaring" coming from the sea
    • If you are near the hazard area of a Tsunami, do not wait for any official orders and find high ground closest to you
  • If you are far from any immediate danger, stay where you are and follow instructions from emergency responders

What to do, during the event:

  • If you are in your house, get out as to prevent getting trapped within your household due to the risen water level
  • If you are in the water, grab onto anything that may float as to prevent potential drowning from the powerful waves (even 6ft waves could drag you under)
  • If you are away from immediate danger, and you are instructed to, evacuate by following designated routes (usually indicated by tsunami warning signs)
  • If you are in a boat out at sea, head towards the waves, but if close to land, head to the nearest port
  • If you are stationed up high to escape the waves and high sea level, remain where you are

How Can You Be Safe After a Tsunami?

How to stay safe afterward:

  • Keep an ear out for any emergency broadcasts with information on the event at hand
  • Stay away from damaged structures for the reason that they may collapse upon top of you
  • Steer clear of any electrical equipment that may be downed in fear of increased electrocution due to water exposure.
  • Remaining flood water may contain hazardous chemicals, may be conducting electricity due to downed power lines, or may be carrying dangerous debris, so it is best to avoid it as much as possible
  • Phone calls may be limited so make sure that you save them to call emergency services for any event that may arise afterward
    • Call your local emergency service if you are either injured are are feeling sick
  • Remain level-headed by communicating with those around you to ease any stress that you may be experiencing
