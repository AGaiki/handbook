What are Landslides?

Landslides are mass movements of debris, rock, and/or earth.   • According to the USGS, there are five types of landslide movement on slopes:
    • Falls: these landslides are abrupt and characterized with large rocks detaching from the slope coming down in free fall. Falls are mainly influenced by gravity, weathering, and water.
    • Topples: these landslides are characterized by rocks or debris coming down in a forward rotation, often caused by gravity or by water in the cracks of the slope.
    • Spreads: these landslides can occur on flat land, not just slopes.
    • Flows: there are different types of flows that vary in speed and danger, such as fast moving debris flows or debris avalanches, or slow moving flows such as a creep flow.
    • Slides: this type of landslide is the kind where the uppermost material (the slide) of the landslide is separated from the more stable underlying material.

Where do Landslides Occur in Region IX?

Landslides happen in all of the United States regions and territories. In Region IX, they are known to occur in the West Coast such as in California, on islands such as Hawaii, and in other states that have mountain ranges such as the Sierra Nevada, Rocky Mountains and Pacific coastal mountain ranges.

What Makes Landslides Dangerous?

Certain kinds of landslides can carry massive amounts of debris, and can accelerate to be as fast as 35 mph according to the USGS. These landslides, also called mudslides, are among the most dangerous kinds of landslides due to the massive debris, such as boulders and cars. These landslides can cause destruction and pose as a danger due to the high speeds and the forces of collision caused by the debris.

How Can you Prepare for a Landslide?

Know the risk in your area:

  • Make a plan with your family and friends in the event of a landslide.
  • Gather any necessary supplies such as non-perishable food and clothes in case you have to evacuate quickly.
  • Sign up for local alert systems, for the Emergency Alert System (EAS) and for the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) weather radio.
  • If you have been told to evacuate or feel unsafe, text SHELTER and your zip code to 43362 (4FEMA) for a list of the nearest shelters.
    • An example of this would be "shelter 54321"
  • Understand the land around you and where landslides could occur or have occurred.
  • Talk to your insurance about what is covered in the event of a landslide.
  • Talk to a geotechnical professional about your property and any risk a landslide and consider taking professional advice to build protective barriers or installing protective measures such as building channels.

Pay attention to any warning signs:

  • Listen to unusual sounds, rushing water, rumbling, and cracking trees.
  • Watch for any rushing water or mud.
  • Watch to see if there are fences, walls, or other objects.
  • Be aware of your area, and see if you are nearby any wildfire burn areas or if there are any large boulders in the landscape (can be a sign that landslides have occurred in the area before).

What Can You Do During a Landslide

Be aware:

  • Listen to your local officials for warnings and alerts.
  • Shelter with friends or family, or evacuate to a public shelter if authorities tell you to.
  • Stay alert and awake if a storm is occurring that could cause a landslide, since most deaths from landslides occur while people are sleeping according to ready.gov.
  • If you are nearby a stream or flowing water, watch to see if the water changes to a muddy brown, since this can be a sign of a landslide coming.

If you are stuck in a landslide:

  • Understand that by the time debris starts flowing, you cannot get away safely.
  • Don't cross bridges if you see flowing debris coming towards you.
  • If you see debris flowing towards you, get to higher ground or uphill as soon as possible.
  • Avoid low places such as dry river beds or river valleys.

How Can You Be Safe After a Landslide?

Protect yourself:

  • Keep away from the slide area, and the debris carried by the landslide.
  • Listen to alerts and updated from local officials.
  • Be aware of any flooding.

Protect others:

  • Report downed power lines, utility lines, damaged roads and railways, and any other potential hazard to authorities.
  • Replant the area affected by the landslide to prevent Flash Floods / Floods.
  • Talk to a geotechnical expert to see what you can do to reduce landslide risks.
  • Let trained professionals examine any damage done to property.
  • Look for any injured people near the slide and direct rescuers accordingly.
