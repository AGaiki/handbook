What are Flash Floods?

According to weather.gov, flash floods are floods that can occur suddenly, or within a 3 to 6 hour time frame. They're often caused by the heavy rainfall that comes with thunderstorms, and the quickness of a flash flood can change depending on the geography of the area.

According to ready.gov, floods can also occur slowly. They can result from snow, rain, storms, and overflow in water systems.

Where do Floods Occur in Region IX?

According to The National Severe Storms Laboratory, flash floods occur in every region and territory in the United States. Even though flash flooding can occur anywhere, certain areas are more prone to flash flooding than others. For instance, urban areas are more prone to flash floods than country or suburban areas due to water being unable to soak into the ground. Floods also occur around rivers, creeks, or other bodies of water. What makes Floods Dangerous?

People can get trapped in their vehicles in the high rising waters, and the powerful waters can cause the vehicles to be washed downstream. People can also drive their vehicles into flooded areas and around barriers and become trapped.

Densely populated areas with freeways, highways, roads, and buildings have a higher potential for flash flooding since the water can't be absorbed into the ground, thus increasing runoff. The excess amount of water can pose as a danger to densely populated areas, especially in places such as:

  • Underground parking garages
  • Underpasses
  • Basements

In places with dry soil and canyons, hikers, residents, and other lifeforms nearby can be trapped by fast moving and rising water. Downstream motion of the water in canyons can also lead to fast moving debris, which can be just as dangerous as the water itself.

Floods can also cause outages, create Landslides, cause issues with transportation, and damage building.

How Can You Prepare For a Flood?

Be ready:

  • Make a plan with your family (know where to go, what to do, what to bring).
  • Gather supplies that you will need in the event of an evacuation, and keep it accessible.
  • Keep important documents and valuables on a higher level or floor and in waterproof containers.
  • Talk with your insurance to find out what is covered in the event of a flood. Consider purchasing/renewing a flood insurance policy.

Keep up to date and know the risk:

  • Sign up for Emergency Alerts and alerts from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration.
  • Be aware of the weather and monitor the possible risk of flooding in your area.
  • Be aware of any alerts in your area.

What Can You Do During a Flood?

Keep up to date:

  • Be aware of any evacuation orders and follow them quickly.
  • Locate the nearest shelter or a safe location in the event of an evacuation.
  • Listen to the NOAA Weather Radio, EAS, or local authorities for alerts, updates, and information.

Protect yourself:

  • Do not drive through flooded areas. Do not drive around barriers. Turn Around. Don't Drown.
  • Do not swim or walk through flooded areas.
  • Avoid being on bridges above rapidly moving water.
  • Get to higher ground if possible.
  • Do not park your car along washes, rivers, streams, creeks, etc.
  • Be very aware and cautious if you have to go out at night, since flood dangers aren't as recognizable.

In the event you are trapped:

  • In a building: get to a higher floor, if not the highest floor, of the building. Don't enclose yourself in a room/attic since the water can continue to rise. If it is absolutely necessary, go to the roof and signal for help.
  • In a car: if your car is being swept away by fast moving waters, do not try to open doors or windows to escape since it can allow water to flood the inside of your vehicle. If water begins to rise inside the vehicle, utilize access to the roof of the car and get on the roof.

How Can You Be Safe After a Flood?

Keep up to date:

  • Listen to radio and local authorities for updates and information. If you have been evacuated, listen to when it is safe to return home.
  • Connect with family and friends and check in with them.

Be cautious:

  • Be aware of any animals or debris that could have flowed into your house.
  • Wear protective clothing to protect yourself during cleanup from animals and debris.
  • Be aware of electrocution and avoid touching wet or exposed electrical equipment if you are in water. Turn off the electricity if it is safe to do so. If you use a generator, keep the generator outside and away from windows.
  • Avoid driving unless if it is an emergency.
  • Avoid wading in water because it can contain debris and chemicals. In some cases, water can be electrified to downed power wires.
