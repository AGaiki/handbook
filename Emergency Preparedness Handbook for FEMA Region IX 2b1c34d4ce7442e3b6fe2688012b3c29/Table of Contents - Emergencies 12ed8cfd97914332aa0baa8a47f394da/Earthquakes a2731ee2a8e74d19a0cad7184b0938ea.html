What are earthquakes?

An earthquake is a natural disaster that occurs when two blocks of earth slip, without much previous warning, past each other across a fault line. It occurs directly under the epicenter, which is the geographical point of origin for the earthquake. Main earthquakes, or mainshocks can sometimes be preceded by foreshocks, which are tinier earthquakes in the same area. After the mainshocks, most earthquakes will be followed by aftershocks, which are usually smaller earthquakes.

Why are Earthquakes Dangerous?

In short, earthquakes cause the earth to shake. Because of this, buildings can become damaged and/or unstable. Furthermore, roads can drop to lower levels than previously. In other cases, the ground below buildings can liquefy (which is the mixing of groundwater and soil) and sink into the ground. All of these actions can be dangerous to human survival. Earthquakes can eventually lead to Flash Floods / Floods, 🔌Power Outages Landslides, and 🌊Tsunamis.

How Can You Prepare for an Earthquake?

Even though earthquakes cannot be predicted, there are quite a few things you can due to ensure that you and your property is safe during an earthquake. Creating an emergency plan is essential to immediate safety. Ready.gov suggests that emergency plans, such as the following, are important:

  • Create a family emergency communications plan, which should include contacts from out-of-state.
  • Plan a spot to meet in the case of separation.
  • Make a supply kit
    • This supply kit can be homemade or can be purchased. To learn more about building your own kit, you can find out here. A general list can include:
      • Non-perishable food
      • Water
      • Cleaning supplies
        • Soap
        • Hand sanitizer.
        • Disinfecting wipes
        • General cleaning supplies
      • First-aid tools
      • Other necessities (such as pet food, medication, etc.)
  • Secure heavy objects, such as:
    • Refrigerators
    • Bookcases
    • Wall-mounted televisions
    • Grandfather clocks
  • Consider purchasing earthquake insurance policy.
  • Even though it is still in heavy development, certain state and federal organizations have developed applications to help with early earthquake notifications. Such applications include the United States Geographical Survey's Shake Alert and California's MyShake.
  • Practice the Drop, Cover, and Hold On maneuver.

What Can You Do During An Earthquake?

During an earthquake, you will start feeling the ground to shake. Objects around you will also start shaking. In the case that you are indoors be sure to follow the Drop, Cover, and Hold On maneuver. If that is not possible, stay in place and cover your head and neck with your arms. Do NOT run outside and avoid doorways.

There may be times when an earthquake strikes and you are in a car. In that case, be sure to pull over and stop. Set your parking break, as the road you are on may become malleable due to the earthquake.

If in bed, turn and lay on your stomach and cover the back of your head and neck with a pillow.

If you find yourself outdoors, stay outdoors and away from buildings and other objects that are prone to becoming unstable (e.g. power lines)

What Can You Do After An Earthquake?

There are many things you can do after an earthquake, such as:

  • Prepare for aftershocks
    • These can come minutes, hours, or even days after the quake. In case of an aftershock, be sure to Drop, Cover, and Hold On.
  • If inside a building, assess building for damage. If the building is damaged, be sure to safely evacuate yourself and others out of the building.
  • Monitor various communication channels. These might include information on temporary shelter (if applicable), other threats (fires, floods, landslides, liquefaction, etc.), and more. Be sure to visit your state / county / city's website to find out more information about notification systems.
  • Be sure to let your contacts (such as family and friends) that you are okay.
  • If you are trapped in a building, bang a metal object or send a text. Shouting is an option, however, using a whistle, if available, is a better option.
  • If you reside in an area where tsunamis, seek higher ground and/or go inland when it is safe to do so.
  • If you see someone that is hurt, help others if you have the training. Be sure to call emergency services, and be the help until help arrives.
